Food & Delicacies

Discover the unique heirloom recipes and local flavors that make Caviteño cuisine one of the most distinct in the Philippines.

Must-Try Local Foods

From the highlands to the coast, Cavite's food scene is as diverse and rich as its history.

Pancit Pusit (Estacion)

A uniquely Caviteño noodle dish made with squid ink that turns the entire dish jet black. The noodles are cooked with squid, pork, and various vegetables, resulting in a savory, slightly briny flavor unlike any pancit in the country. It is considered the signature dish of Cavite City.

Where to try: Panciteria sa Cavite City, Cavite City Public Market area

Bulalo

Tagaytay's most celebrated dish — a slow-cooked beef bone marrow and shank soup that is deeply rich, hearty, and warming. The cold highland climate of Tagaytay makes a steaming bowl of bulalo the ultimate comfort meal. Marrow is scooped directly from the bone for a uniquely indulgent experience.

Where to try: Mahogany Market, Leslie's Restaurant, Bag of Beans — Tagaytay

Fried Tawilis

Tawilis is a small freshwater sardine found only in Taal Lake, making it exclusive to the Cavite–Batangas area. Deep-fried until golden and crispy, it is served as a snack or meal with steamed rice and vinegar dipping sauce. The crispy fins and delicate flesh are considered a delicacy and a must-try for every visitor to Tagaytay.

Where to try: Mahogany Market, Talisay town proper, Tagaytay fish stalls

Kapeng Barako

The legendary "Barako" coffee of Cavite is made from Coffea liberica beans grown in the highlands of Amadeo and Silang. Known for its intensely bold, bitter flavor with a distinctly strong aroma, Barako is brewed and served black or with just a touch of muscovado sugar. It is the cultural drink of Cavite and one of the rarest coffee varieties in the world.

Where to try: Amadeo coffee farms, Café de Lipa (Tagaytay branch), local farm cafes in Silang

Bibingka Espesyal

A premium version of the traditional Filipino rice cake, Cavite's bibingka espesyal is made with extra salted duck eggs, cheese, and coconut cream, then baked in banana leaf-lined clay pots over live coals. The result is a golden, slightly charred exterior and a soft, cheesy interior. It is widely sold during the Christmas season and at local pasalubong shops year-round.

Where to try: Roadside karinderyas in Tagaytay, pasalubong centers in Kawit and Dasmariñas

Sineguelas Jam & Preserves

Made from sineguelas (Spanish plum), this sweet-sour jam is a Cavite specialty found in highland areas. The fruit thrives in the province's cool climate and is harvested seasonally. Sineguelas jam is spread on bread or used as a condiment, and is one of the most beloved pasalubong items that visitors bring home from Tagaytay and Silang markets.

Where to try: Pasalubong shops along Aguinaldo Highway, Tagaytay weekend markets

Sampalok Candy

A traditional Filipino candy made from tamarind (sampalok) coated in sugar, these sweet-sour treats are a classic pasalubong from Cavite. The candies are handmade in small batches and found in local markets and roadside stalls throughout the province. Their bright, tangy flavor is an irresistible treat for all ages.

Where to try: Public markets in Cavite City, Kawit, and Tagaytay pasalubong stalls

Puto & Kutchinta

Soft, steamed rice cakes are a staple in Caviteño markets and celebrations. Puto (white, fluffy steamed cakes) and kutchinta (sticky, orange-brown cakes) are enjoyed as breakfast or merienda with freshly grated coconut and butter. In Cavite, these are often made with special local rice varieties and sold by the dozens at morning markets and fiestas.

Where to try: Morning markets in all municipalities, especially Imus and Dasmariñas wet markets
